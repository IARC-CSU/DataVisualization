# Nose, sinuses etc. (code 19, C30–31)

## Quick take
Nose and paranasal-sinus cancer is the clearest "vanishing occupational cancer" in CI5 — almost every registry falls, often to near-zero. What remains is a surprisingly rich geography of surviving risk: Australian Indigenous populations, US Southwest Native Americans, and — still — a striking Italian male-dominant signal.

## What catches the eye

- **Italy has some of the largest male-to-female ratios in the whole dataset.** Parma vol VIII 63:1, Basilicata vol XII 52:1, Avellino vol XII 33:1, Macerata vol VIII 25:1. Global vol-VIII median ratio is ~1.9:1. Almost certainly a surviving occupational signal — wood dust in furniture-making regions, leather dust in tanneries.
- **Australia Northern Territory Indigenous women: 3.30 in vol X** — 10.6× the world median, the top spatial outlier for this cancer. Men 2.94 (5× median). Likely reflects biomass-smoke / chronic sinusitis exposures.
- **Jamaica Kingston men show a clear inverted-U peaking early** (vol III, 3.01) then falling to 0.08 (vol XI) — 93 % down. One of the cleanest historical declines.
- **Shanghai men fell 2.94 → 0.35 (−88 %).** The urbanisation + workplace-ventilation story familiar from other aerodigestive sites.
- **The generalised decline is near-universal.** Most "biggest decrease" rows are near-zeros; many vol-XII female rates in US-Japanese and US-Chinese subgroups are literally 0.00.
- **An unusual bulge of female-heavy ratios in Iran Golestan, Jamaica and some Caribbean registries** — 5-to-7:1 female:male for nose/sinus cancer. Worth checking: is this a real signal (biomass cook-smoke exposure) or a small-number artefact? The numerators are small.
- **Italy Sondrio women: +1000 % rise** (0.12 → 1.32). One of very few female-rising registries for this site.

## Caveats
- Numbers are small almost everywhere — many of the "rises" are from 0.05 to 0.5 per 100 000, so relative changes exaggerate.
- The ICD-10 C30–C31 range excludes nasopharynx (C11). Don't conflate with the Asian-Chinese EBV-driven NPC signal.
- Ethnic subgroup rows in US data are noisy due to small sub-populations; the Chinese-Hawaiian women's 3.90 in vol II is based on relatively few cases.

## Worth a deeper look
- **A paper specifically on surviving occupational sinonasal cancer in Italian furniture and leather-making regions** would be publishable — the Italian numbers are outsized and consistent.
- Australian Northern Territory Indigenous rates for nose/sinus are under-discussed; sits nicely with a broader Indigenous-Australia upper-airway story.
- The apparent female-heavy signal in biomass-exposed populations (Iran Golestan, Caribbean) is worth testing in larger datasets — could be a novel indoor-air-pollution signature.
