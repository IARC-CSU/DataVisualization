# Placenta (code 37, C58)

## Quick take
Placental / gestational trophoblastic tumours are rare in the West (ASR typically < 0.1) but CI5 shows a surprisingly strong Asian / Indigenous / Sub-Saharan signal that echoes older clinical literature on molar-pregnancy incidence. Almost all countries show decline; the outliers are geography *and* ethnic sub-populations.

## What catches the eye

- **USA New Mexico American-Indian women, vol III: ASR 8.71, 35× the world median.** This is by far the top spatial outlier for the site — a historic finding and one that has not been revisited much in recent decades. Worth re-checking with subsequent SEER Native American subgroups.
- **Vietnam Hanoi women, vol VII (1.85) and vol VIII (2.22): 20× median.** The Southeast-Asian gestational-trophoblastic-disease belt is visible in CI5 with strong numbers.
- **Argentina Chaco (1.61), India Poona (2.68), Nigeria Ibadan (3.08)** — all consistent with the historical geography of molar pregnancy.
- **The disease has essentially disappeared in the West.** Multiple high-income registries reach 0.00 in vol XII (Canada NWT, Canada Yukon, Hungary, Algeria Sétif, Iceland). Likely driven by earlier first pregnancy + better pregnancy outcomes + folate.
- **Early-age peak at age band 4–5 (15–24 yr)** in virtually every large registry — exactly where gestational trophoblastic disease is biologically expected.
- **No signals in men, which is as it should be.**

## Caveats
- Very small case counts for almost every registry-volume cell; the magnitude of "top outlier" entries here is sensitive to one or two cases.
- Gestational trophoblastic tumours have coding variability across ICD-8/9/10 — some may hide under C79 (metastatic) historically.
- Most of the late-volume zero rates reflect genuine rarity plus small registries; do not over-interpret "collapse".

## Worth a deeper look
- The Vietnam Hanoi + Argentina Chaco + Indian-subcontinent cluster maps the historical geography of hydatidiform mole well — a coherent descriptive piece could revive attention to the topic.
- The US Native-American 1970s signal (New Mexico vol III) is interesting because of its extreme magnitude; a quick cross-check with the contemporary Native-American-specific SEER subset would reveal whether it persists.
